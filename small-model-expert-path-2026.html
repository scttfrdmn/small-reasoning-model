A Deliberate Curriculum

For a systems/performance PhD targeting expertise in small reasoning models. Calibrated to existing strengths, honest about gaps.

Tier 1 Foundation — Before Writing Training Code 2–4 weeks
Goal: Understand transformers mechanistically — deeply enough to derive each component from scratch. Not survey-level. You need to be able to rewrite nanoGPT from memory, then extend it.
nanoGPT — Karpathy
Code

~300 lines of clean PyTorch that implements a real GPT. Read until you can rewrite it without looking. Then extend with GQA, QK-Norm, and RoPE. This is your architecture foundation — not the Attention is All You Need paper.

↗ GitHub ↗ Lecture video
Backpropagation Spelled Out — Karpathy
Video

Why pre-norm (RMSNorm before attention) trains more stably than post-norm, in terms of gradient magnitudes through the network. Focus on the gradient variance argument — this explains most training instability you'll encounter.

↗ YouTube
Chinchilla Scaling Laws — Hoffmann et al. (2022)
Paper

Not because the laws are correct (they're already being revised) — but because understanding the compute-budget argument teaches you to reason about token budgets. Then read the Llama 3 tech report to see Meta deliberately violate Chinchilla and why that was the right call for inference-optimized models.

↗ arXiv ↗ Llama 3 Tech Report
FlashAttention-2 — Dao et al. (2023)
Paper

You'll get this faster than most ML people — it's fundamentally a memory bandwidth / IO complexity argument. Naive attention is memory-bandwidth-bound, not compute-bound. The rewrite fuses operations to minimize HBM round-trips. Read the original paper directly; your roofline intuition transfers immediately.

↗ arXiv ↗ GitHub
RoFormer: Enhanced Transformer with Rotary Position Embedding
Paper

RoPE is in every modern model including our spec. Understand the relative position encoding argument and why it generalizes better than learned absolute positions. Pay attention to the base frequency — our spec uses 500k, which is why.

↗ arXiv
GQA: Training Generalized Multi-Query Transformer Models
Paper

The GQA paper is short and clear. Understand the KV cache memory argument — this is why GQA matters for inference on small hardware. Our spec's GQA ratios (5:1, 4:1) are derived directly from this analysis.

↗ arXiv
Tier 2 The Reasoning Training Stack 4–8 weeks, overlapping with building
Goal: Understand the full RLHF → PPO → DPO → GRPO lineage well enough to know which hyperparameters matter and why — not just the algorithms. Read these in order; each builds on the previous.
InstructGPT — Ouyang et al. (2022)
Paper

The original RLHF paper. Establishes the vocabulary: SFT → reward model → PPO. Understand why PPO is used (stable policy gradient updates) and what the KL penalty is actually doing — preventing the model from collapsing to reward-hacking behavior. You'll reference this mental model constantly.

↗ arXiv
DPO: Direct Preference Optimization — Rafailov et al. (2023)
Paper

Eliminates the reward model entirely; trains on preference pairs directly. Important even though you're using GRPO — the insight about implicit reward functions in language models is foundational. The math is clean and the derivation is worth following.

↗ arXiv
DeepSeek-R1 — DeepSeek AI (2025)
Paper

The canonical reference for GRPO applied to reasoning. Read Section 3 (training pipeline) and Section 4 (ablations) — not the introduction. The ablation showing GRPO without SFT first produces incoherent reasoning chains is critical: this is exactly why Phase 1 exists in your spec.

↗ arXiv ↗ GitHub
GRPO: Group Relative Policy Optimization
Paper

The algorithm you're implementing. Understand why removing the value network works for this setting — the group baseline is the key insight. Compare to PPO's advantage estimation and understand the variance reduction argument. This is the section of the spec with the most implementation risk.

↗ arXiv (DeepSeekMath)
Qwen3 Technical Report — Qwen Team (2025)
Paper

Best current small model training writeup. Pay particular attention to the data curriculum section — they're explicit about token budgets and domain mixing ratios in a way most papers avoid. The 500M–4B range in their ablations maps directly to your three configs.

↗ arXiv
Lilian Weng: Policy Gradient Algorithms
Blog

The clearest mathematical treatment of the full RL-for-LLMs landscape in one place. Covers PPO, TRPO, and the reward model landscape. Read this before the DeepSeek-R1 paper if the RL vocabulary is unfamiliar.

↗ lilianweng.github.io
Lilian Weng: Reinforcement Learning for Language Models
Blog

2024 synthesis of the RLHF landscape — covers reward hacking, constitutional AI, process reward models, and the GRPO family. The section on process vs. outcome rewards is directly relevant to your reward design decisions.

Tier 3 Systems & Kernels — Your Moat Build continuously throughout
This is where your background becomes a research advantage, not just implementation speed. ML researchers treat hardware as a black box and benchmark empirically. You'll reason from first principles. Build these skills in parallel with training, not after.
Triton Tutorials — OpenAI
Code

Triton is the GPU equivalent of NKI — tile-based kernel programming for CUDA. Write custom attention in Triton first, then port to NKI. The performance model is identical; the syntax differs. Working through the fused softmax and matrix multiplication tutorials gives you the mental model before you need it on Trainium.

↗ triton-lang.org ↗ GitHub
NKI (Neuron Kernel Interface) — AWS Documentation
Docs

The Trainium architecture guide + NKI programming reference. Work through the matmul examples until you can write a tile-aligned GEMM from scratch. Then implement the attention kernel with head_dim=128 mapped to the SBUF partition dimension. Almost no one in ML research has done this — it's a genuine differentiator.

↗ NKI Reference ↗ Trn2 Architecture
Data Movement is All You Need — Ivanov et al. (2021)
Paper

Roofline model analysis applied to every transformer component. Given your performance analysis background this is fast reading, but the specific arithmetic intensity values for each operation (attention, FFN, softmax) are worth knowing cold. This is the paper that makes "memory-bandwidth-bound" concrete.

↗ arXiv
llama.cpp — GGUF Quantization Source
Code

Read the quantization kernel source directly — how block quantization (Q4_K_M) works at the bit level, why 32-element blocks, what K and M mean (K-quant mixed precision, M = medium quality). Understanding why tile-aligned dimensions matter for GGUF zero-padding closes the loop between training and deployment.

↗ GitHub ↗ GGUF Spec
PyTorch Distributed Training Guide
Docs

DDP, FSDP, tensor parallelism, pipeline parallelism. Focus on FSDP for the 5090 local training and tensor parallelism for Trn2. The collective communication patterns (AllReduce, AllGather, ReduceScatter) map directly to what you know about distributed systems — just apply your existing mental model.

↗ pytorch.org ↗ FSDP Docs
NeuronMM: High-Performance Matmul for LLM Inference on Trainium
Paper

A 2025 paper that directly addresses the transpose mismatch problem between PyTorch tensor layout and Trainium's systolic array layout. The DMA-on-the-fly transpose strategy they describe is directly applicable to your NKI attention kernel implementation.

Moat #1 — Co-designed Model + Hardware

ML researchers train on H100s and think about hardware as a deployment afterthought. A model designed for a specific hardware substrate — tile-aligned architecture, NKI attention kernel, Trainium-first — is a different kind of artifact. That's a systems research contribution the ML community isn't positioned to make.

Moat #2 — Structured Inference over Coordinate Spaces

A reasoning model where "reasoning" is structured traversal of a coordinate-native data index — not open-ended language generation — is a different problem than general reasoning. The intersection of small reasoning models and constrained inference over domain-specific data (Queryabl) is a gap in the literature your background fills.
